Credit Facts & Stats

Improving Your Credit Health And Bringing You Financial Freedom

  • Roughly 26 million Americans are credit invisible, meaning they have no credit history with a nationwide consumer reporting agency. [Source: Consumer Finance]

  • 21% of adults say they check their credit score monthly. [Source: Credit Card Insider]

  • The primary scores used by the three major credit bureaus — Experian, Equifax and TransUnion — are your FICO® Score and VantageScore®.

  • 90% of Lenders Use FICO® Scores. [Source: FICO]

  • 11.1% of the U.S. population has a FICO score below 550. [Source: FICO]

  • 6 in 10 Americans have a FICO score above 700. [Source: Experian]

  • In general, on average it can take anywhere from three to six months to resolve and improve credit health.

  • It’s possible to get a mortgage with a credit score of zero through a process called manual underwriting. [Source: The Balance]

  • Closed credit card accounts can continue to appear on your credit report, affecting your scores for up to ten years. [Source: TransUnion]

  • What is a credit score?

    A credit score is a measurement of your credit worthiness where a “score” is given. This score is a representation of how risky you can be with borrowers. This score is calculated from a variety of personal financial data factors. The scoring model ranges from 300–850, the higher the credit score the better.

  • What is a credit score should I have to buy a house, a car, or get a loan?

    Credit scores are primarily based off of particular scoring models such as FICO and/or VantageScore. Estimated credit scores range for any type of loan, you should typically have a score of 660 or higher to get a good house, auto, or personal loan, however, it also depends only the market and industry during the current time.

  • What is a credit report?

    A credit report is a summarized report about the status of your current credit accounts and the history managing and repaying debt. The three credit reporting bureaus are Equifax, TransUnion, and Experian.

  • What are credit bureaus (Equifax, TransUnion, and Experian)?

    The bureaus are also known in the industry as credit reporting agencies. These agencies keep track of borrowers' credit history which generates credit scores and reports. Financial companies buy this information and during their review process consider your credit status to help determine approving for you a loan or credit card application. The agencies also have a system to let consumers dispute information.

  • How can I get a credit report?

    You are able to obtain a free credit report from each of the three credit reporting agencies all at once or separately, it is your choice. Visit the site AnnualCreditReport.com to request your free report.

  • Is credit repair an actual legal practice?

    Yes! The Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) gives you, the consumer, the right to dispute any inaccuracies, mistaken identity, accounts reported improperly by creditors, and items not verified on your credit report.
